Machine Yearning

The first BUILD homework assignment involved a free drawing exercise using AxiDraw and Python. It was a unique experience that allowed me to channel my creativity through computation and code.
My motivation to create an artwork came from a desire to revisit the past-- after a class session on Reflexive Writing-- where I had reread a journal entry that I had written before leaving for Canberra.

A snippet of my journal entry from January 14th 2023. Written at Structure Coffee, Montréal, QC

This prompted me to reflect on my personal journey, which sparked a desire to further immortalise a memory of a past.

With this poem in hand, I wanted to somehow use this, or at least an element of the poem, for the fortnight 1 BUILD homework. Due to the time constraint, I had to choose a snippet of the poem to use, and I chose the last stanza -- my personal favourite stanza -- of the poem.

I then realized that manually plotting each letter using the Latin alphabet would be too time-consuming, so I looked for an alternative method that would allow me to represent the poem with minimal code. Eventually, I settled on using ASCII representation, where each letter is represented by pairs of zeroes and ones.

To do this, I created two function to plot zero and one, and then created functions to plot each of the four possible pairs: 00, 01, 10, and 11.


Machine Yearning (2023)
pen on paper
5.8 x 8.3 in

Machine Yearning is an artwork consisting of five words represented in binary that depicts a crazy lover's visceral yearning for the beloved.

The text reads:

beloved, 
  
  until then
  
  haunt me
  
                      haunt  me (x10)

It is a story about separation, wherein the mad lover in this story begs for the beloved to haunt her until then comes around.

To elucidate the sense of madness, the binary strings on the right side are ten randomised loops of the phrase "haunt me". Here, the lover is clearly consumed by the ever-consuming force of yearning for the beloved.

This line was heavily inspired by a line from Emily Brontë's Wuthering Heights:

"You said I killed you, Haunt me, then"

I'm particularly drawn to thinkers, writers, scholars and artists who boldly celebrate and acknowledge the immense power of emotions. Among them are Sappho, Virginia Woolf, FKA Twigs, and Sophie Parnok. At its core, to feel is to be human, and in a world that often values stoicism, rationality, and positivism, we risk losing touch with what truly defines us.

If we want to integrate humanity into technology, we have to recognize and embrace the essential qualities that make us human - namely, the sheer beauty and raw power of emotions; something that has moved and motivated humans to create great works throughout time. By acknowledging and incorporating this aspect of humanity into our technological advancements, we are carrying on the tradition of what it means to be human, a tradition that has endured through time and space.
